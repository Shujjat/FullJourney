**Detailed Response**
Coulomb's Law is named after Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, a French physicist who discovered the relationship between two charged particles in the 18th century. The law states that the force (F) between two point charges (q1 and q2) is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance (r) between them.

Mathematically, Coulomb's Law can be expressed as:

F = k \* (q1 \* q2) / r^2

Where k is a constant that depends on the medium surrounding the charged particles (such as air or vacuum). In free space, k = 8.99 × 10^9 N m^2 C^-2.

**Examples**

1. Two positively charged particles with charges +3 μC and +4 μC are separated by a distance of 5 cm. Calculate the force between them.
Using Coulomb's Law, we can calculate the force as:

F = k \* (q1 \* q2) / r^2
= 8.99 × 10^9 N m^2 C^-2 \* (3 × 10^-6 C \* 4 × 10^-6 C) / (0.05 m)^2
≈ 5.32 × 10^-7 N

The force between the two positively charged particles is approximately 5.32 × 10^-7 Newtons.

2. A negatively charged particle with a charge of -2 μC is located 8 cm away from a positively charged particle with a charge of +6 μC. Calculate the force between them.
Using Coulomb's Law, we can calculate the force as:

F = k \* (q1 \* q2) / r^2
= 8.99 × 10^9 N m^2 C^-2 \* (-2 × 10^-6 C \* 6 × 10^-6 C) / (0.08 m)^2
≈ -3.39 × 10^-6 N

The force between the negatively charged particle and the positively charged particle is approximately -3.39 × 10^-6 Newtons.

**Related Questions with Answers**

Q: What are the units of the constant k in Coulomb's Law?
A: The units of k are N m^2 C^-2 (Newton-meters squared per coulomb).

Q: What happens when two charges with the same sign are separated by a distance?
A: According to Coulomb's Law, the force between two charges with the same sign is repulsive, meaning it pushes them apart.
